It's often as an experience, not as a memory. Doctors Judith Herman and Bessel Van Der Kolk both recited experiences with patients in their books, in which the traumatic event slowly manifested themselves in the patient's memories in the forms of images and bodily sensations. In fact, it's unheard of for a person with PTSD to experience this aspect of their trauma in a manner resembling a coherent, organized verbal narrative. Something Omori takes into account.
Dr. Van Der Kolk writes "The imprints of traumatic experiences are organized not as coherent logical narratives, but in fragmented sensory and emotional traces: images, sounds, and physical sensations". We can see these in the phobia fight scenes that happen across the three days before Sunny's move. Omori is afraid of:



Spiders

Heights

Drowning





It's in the real world that we come face to face with the embodiment of these fears.


It's this same Something that becomes an important light motif throughout the course of Omori, showing up when something vaguely related to Sunny's trauma is implied. As Sunny suffers panic attacks over these fears, more and more clues about the events constituting his trauma are brought into the light, except, they still say nothing. They affect Sunny negatively, but just like something itself, it isn't understood why. It isn't until we've seen the whole truth that we are able to consider alongside Sunny what the significance of these images and sensations are.


Omori captures the consequences of this concept quite well too. We still experience fear when engaging in the panic attacks over his traumatic related phobias. Even if we can't explain why things like spiders, heights, and drowning terrify us. The game puts us in Sunny's position epistemologically and emotionally. Operating under the guise of a third-person perspective, the narrative conceals that fact it's actually operating in the second person. If Omori doesn't quite understand what something means yet, then neither do we. The game pays special attention to several techniques meant to make us react to these situations with the same feelings of dread and despair that Sunny is shown to experience. Actual things like the music, visual elements, and actual gameplay of particular scenes make us feel very much the same way Sunny does. This is employed time and time again to add suspense and immersion to the narrative.
